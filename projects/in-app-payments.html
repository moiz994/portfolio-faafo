The Challenge

Mobile learners on edX couldn't complete a course purchase inside the app. They had to drop out to a browser, authenticate again, and complete a multi-step web checkout — a friction-heavy flow that was losing conversions at scale. With millions of mobile users, the gap between mobile traffic and mobile revenue was significant.

The opportunity was clear: bring the purchase experience natively into the app, and reduce the steps between intent and payment to as few taps as possible.

Approach

I led the product definition for both the iOS (Apple Pay) and Android (Google Pay) implementations in parallel. This meant working across three teams: mobile engineering, payments infrastructure, and the edX commerce platform — each with its own constraints, timelines, and approval requirements from Apple and Google.

Platform review requirements were the trickiest constraint. Both stores have specific rules around in-app purchasing flows for digital content. I mapped out the compliance requirements early and built them into the spec, so engineering wasn't discovering edge cases mid-build.

I also worked with the data team to define conversion tracking from the start — we needed to be able to attribute revenue correctly from day one, not retrofit analytics after launch.

Outcome

In-app payments launched across 3,000+ courses on both platforms. The new revenue stream generated over $700,000 in its first year — money that wouldn't have existed without removing the mobile checkout friction.

It was a clean example of a product change where the outcome was fully measurable: we opened a revenue channel that previously didn't exist, and the number speaks for itself.
