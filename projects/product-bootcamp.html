The Challenge

Arbisoft is an engineering-first company. The talent is there, the technical skills are strong — but there was no systematic way for engineers, designers, and analysts to develop product thinking. Good product instincts existed in pockets, but there was no shared vocabulary, no structured process, and no culture of proposing and validating new product ideas.

The goal was to change that — not through hiring, but through building the capability internally. Design a program that could teach product fundamentals, excite people about the discipline, and generate real product bets as an output.

Approach

I designed the Bootcamp from scratch: curriculum, format, facilitation, and the hackathon structure. The content was built around three core workshops — problem framing, opportunity sizing, and concept validation — each with applied exercises rather than lecture-style delivery.

The culminating hackathon was the real test. Teams had 48 hours to take a problem space, run a compressed discovery process, and pitch a product concept. The judging criteria were intentionally strict: we weren't rewarding ideas, we were rewarding evidence of structured thinking.

I also had to run this alongside my existing project load. The Bootcamp was a side initiative that had to earn its time — which meant every session had to be tight, high-quality, and worth the participants' investment.

Outcome

The Bootcamp ran with 60+ participants across 3 workshops and a full-day hackathon. The energy exceeded expectations — people who had never thought of themselves as product thinkers were pitching structured, evidence-backed concepts.

Most importantly, 2 product bets were greenlit for further development from the hackathon output. The Bootcamp created a repeatable model for product culture-building at Arbisoft — and established the expectation that product thinking is everyone's responsibility, not just the PM's.
