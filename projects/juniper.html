The Challenge

Arbisoft had no structured channel for product ideas to surface, get evaluated, and gain organisational traction. Good ideas were either lost in Slack threads, trapped in individual heads, or never made it past informal hallway conversations. There was no mechanism for a junior engineer's insight to reach the right decision-maker.

The challenge was twofold: build a platform that solved this, and demonstrate that a product manager could build and ship software independently — proving the viability of AI-assisted development as a real capability, not just a promise.

Approach

I built Juniper entirely solo using AI-assisted development tools. No dedicated engineers. No sprint team. Just me, a clear spec, and a disciplined build process over two weeks.

The platform was designed around a simple workflow: employees submit ideas, ideas get structured through a lightweight process, and the best ones surface to product and leadership for evaluation. I kept the feature set ruthlessly small — the goal was adoption, not sophistication.

Internally, I treated this as a product experiment with two hypotheses: (1) people have ideas they want to share if there's a low-friction way to do it, and (2) AI-assisted dev is production-capable for a PM with the right process. Both turned out to be true.

Outcome

Juniper launched on day one to 200+ employees. The platform was immediately adopted — people had ideas queued up and ready to submit. Within the first month, it had surfaced several concepts that went into formal product evaluation.

Beyond the product itself, Juniper established Arbisoft's internal product incubation function — the organisational muscle for turning internal ideas into potential product bets. It also became a reference point for what AI-assisted solo development could deliver at professional quality.
